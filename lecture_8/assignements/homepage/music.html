Music

During my whole life, I've loved listening to music. Of course, as a child, I was introduced to it through my parents’ tapes and CDs, so I ended up listening to a lot of rock-pop bands like The Police, U2, Dire Straits, Queen, and Red Hot Chili Peppers. Besides these artists, my parents were also fans of Zucchero, an Italian soul singer, and the musical The Blues Brothers, which helped broaden my musical background since I got to know soul and blues as well.


Out of pride for the region of Italy where I grew up, when I was around 10 years old, I started listening, on my own initiative, to folk songs that were born during the World Wars, which were fought in the areas I grew up in—particularly WWI. I became obsessed with a CD called Canti di Montagna (Mountain Chants).


Middle school went by quickly, and my musical interest shifted towards the pop/dance wave of the early 2010s. I have to admit that the social pressure to fit in with friends and classmates largely drove my music choices during that period, but I can’t deny that there were some memorable tracks I still enjoy today, such as California Gurls, Memories, and Time of Our Lives... but let's not kid ourselves, here is the elephant in the room:


In high school, I gradually started regaining some independence in my music tastes. In total antithesis to the music I listened to in previous years, I started alternating between Sgt. Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band and Abbey Road. I also dabbled in psychedelic rock with Jefferson Airplane and the hard rock of AC/DC. During this time, I rediscovered the one band that is probably my favorite right now: the Dire Straits. I’m in love with Making Movies and Love Over Gold, but I could listen to Brothers in Arms forever.


In the last year of high school, I made peace with myself and realized it’s okay to be influenced by global trends. I started to give more space and time to indie and rap—two genres that, in Italy, were still underground at the time. I first got into these genres with two specific albums: Mainstream and Hellvisback. I then delved deeper into the Italian scene, discovering and falling in love with many other artists like Frah Quintale, the members of the Lovegang126 clique, Marracash, and Gué Pequeno.


During my university years, I realized that there isn’t really a genre of music I dislike; rather, there are artists I enjoy and others that bore me. I began to explore pretty much every angle and shade of music—from jazz (Duke Ellington & John Coltrane, The Cuban Tapes) to electronic (You’ve Come a Long Way, Baby, Berlin Calling), and even reggaeton (YHLQMDLG). Nevertheless, I found that hip-hop and rock ’n’ roll were the worlds I most enjoyed exploring. I dedicated a lot of time to understanding their roots and where they intersect. When I consciously heard Killing in the Name for the first time, it felt like an epiphany. Funnily enough, it happened while I was on vacation with my sisters in Lisbon, where we stumbled upon a Neuroethology conference and a cover band was performing it on ukuleles. From there, I dove into the entire discography of Rage Against the Machine and Beastie Boys. Not far from this wave is a playlist I’ve curated according to my taste, also influenced by Breaking Bad, as you can probably tell.
